THE IRON HORDE
Rise of the Barbarian Chieftains: A History of the Varak Confederation Wars
Written by Archivist Nikephoros of the Byzantra Military Academy
Compiled from Field Reports, Survivor Accounts, and Border Station Records

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

FOREWORD

This book began as a military report. It became something more.

The events described here — the three great northern incursions, the failed campaign of General Bardanes, the eventual treaty of the Coldwater Crossing — were traumatic for the border provinces and produced considerable paperwork, most of which I have now read. What I found in that paperwork was not what I expected.

I expected accounts of savagery. I found accounts of people.

The northerners who came south in the First Incursion were not driven by bloodlust, as the popular accounts have it. They were driven by a famine that killed a third of the Greymount clan's livestock in one winter. The Second Incursion was retaliation for a border incident in which imperial soldiers burned a clan waystation while its inhabitants were inside. The Third was more complicated, and I will deal with it in its place.

History looks different when you read it from both sides of the border.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART ONE: THE FIRST INCURSION

In the Year of the Iron Winter, the Greymount and Ironfoot clans crossed the Coldwater River in force. It was early spring — earlier than any border garrison expected, because the crossing required ice that should not have lasted so long. The ice lasted because the winter had been brutal even by northern standards.

The border garrison at Coldwater Station numbered four hundred. The clan force was estimated, from the aftermath, at around three thousand. The station was overrun in a single morning.

What followed was not the wholesale slaughter that the popular accounts describe. The clans took food. They took livestock. They took some captives — mostly young adults, healthy ones, who would be ransomed or integrated into clan life according to Varak custom. They did not burn the station. They did not kill the garrison commander, one Captain Paulinos, who survived to write a meticulous report that became the primary source for this chapter.

Captain Paulinos wrote: "They moved with discipline. There was a war-chief directing them — a tall woman, scarred on the right cheek, whose word was immediately obeyed. They were not looting. They were provisioning. When the granaries were emptied and the cattle were gathered, they stopped. They did not touch the officers' quarters, which I initially attributed to mercy and later understood was simply because officers' quarters do not contain food."

The clan force was across the river and back in their own territory within four days. They left behind no bodies except their own dead, whom they carried.

Byzantra's response was to send General Bardanes north with eight thousand soldiers to "pacify the border region" — which is the military vocabulary for a punitive expedition against people whose territory you do not actually have the ability to occupy.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART TWO: THE BARDANES DISASTER

General Bardanes was a competent commander with extensive experience fighting organized armies on open ground. The northern territories are not open ground and the clan warriors were not an organized army in any sense he had prepared for.

He advanced north in good order. He found no one to fight. The clans had simply pulled back from the areas he was marching through, taking everything moveable with them.

He could not feed his army off the land he was crossing — there was nothing there. He had supply lines stretching two hundred miles back to the border. Those supply lines ran through mountain passes. In late spring, those passes are controlled by the weather and, as it turned out, by small clan units who had been watching the supply caravans for two weeks before they started burning them.

Bardanes fought a number of inconclusive skirmishes against enemies who appeared, struck, and disappeared. He won all the skirmishes, in the sense that the clan units withdrew each time. He lost, in a more meaningful sense, about forty men per week to ambush, exposure, and the illness that follows when an army's food supply is degraded.

He pulled back across the Coldwater River after six weeks. He had achieved nothing, lost roughly two hundred men, and left the clans with a supply of imperial weapons, equipment, and provisions from the burned caravans that kept them comfortable through the following winter.

His after-action report is a masterpiece of understated acknowledgment of failure. The sentence I find most affecting: "The enemy was always present and never findable. This is a state of affairs for which our tactical doctrine has no adequate prescription."

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART THREE: THE SECOND INCURSION AND THE TREATY

The Second Incursion came three years later. It was larger, better organized, and drove forty miles deep into imperial territory before the border legions caught and engaged it.

The engagement at Three Oaks Field lasted two days and ended in what the military records call "a tactical victory" for the empire. By this they mean: the clan force withdrew, leaving the field to the legions. They do not mention that the legions had taken three thousand casualties, that the retreating clan force was in good order, or that the Varak Confederation's war-chiefs left the battle appearing, by all accounts, satisfied.

The treaty negotiation that followed was long and irritating for all parties.

The Varak Confederation wanted: recognition of the Coldwater River as the permanent border; compensation for the burned waystation; trade rights for Varak goods in the border markets; and what the treaty document calls "a formal acknowledgment by the Byzantra Empire that the First Incursion was a response to famine conditions and not an act of unprovoked aggression."

The Byzantra Empire wanted: the return of the Captain Paulinos (who had been ransomed, returned safely, and was personally in favour of the treaty); a guarantee against future incursions; and the return of the weapons from the burned caravans, which the Varak politely declined as they had been used already.

The final treaty gave both parties most of what they wanted and was honoured, on both sides, for the next forty years. This makes it one of the more successful border treaties in the empire's history, which says something about the quality of border treaties generally.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

AFTERWORD

I began this as a military report. I am finishing it as something else — I am not sure what to call it.

The northerners are not who we say they are in the popular accounts. They are also not without violence and cruelty — the clan wars that preceded the Confederation's formation were brutal by any standard. I am not writing an apology.

I am writing an account of people who had reasons for what they did, who fought cleverly when they had to and made peace when it served them, and who were considerably less alien than the border propaganda suggested.

The border is quieter now than it has been in a century. On both sides, people trade and occasionally intermarry and raise children who have two languages and uncertain loyalties and, sometimes, a broader understanding of the world than either of their parents.

This seems good to me.

— Archivist Nikephoros, Nikomedeia
