SONGS OF THE NORTHERN CLANS
Barbarian Sagas, Battle Chants, and Hearth Tales of the Varak Confederation
Collected by Borderman and Scholar Dmitrios Ouroumenos
In the Years of the Third Northern Peace

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

COLLECTOR'S NOTE

I was born on the border. My mother was from Nikomedeia. My father was from one of the smaller Varak clans — a man who had settled in the border towns when he was young and learned to trade rather than raid. Growing up between two worlds, I learned early that the people my civilized neighbours called "barbarians" had things worth knowing and that the word "barbarian" meant, to those using it, simply: people who are not like us.

These songs and tales were given to me freely by the hearth-singers of the Varak Confederation — the women and men whose role in clan life is to remember. They sing the old songs. They tell the old stories. They carry the history of a people that has no writing in their mouths and lungs instead.

Some of what follows is violent. Some is tender. Some is both at once. I have translated as faithfully as I can, which means accepting that there are concepts in the northern languages for which I have no perfect words in ours.

THE FIRST CHANT: THE MAKING OF THE CLANS

In the beginning there was cold.
Not winter-cold, not mountain-cold:
the cold that was before fire,
before fur, before the first warm breath.

From that cold the First Mother walked out.
She walked until she was warm.
The warmth she left behind
became the clans.

This is why we build the fire first.
This is why we put the oldest woman closest to the heat.
This is why when a clan dies, we say:
the cold took them back.

THE SAGA OF RED-HAND TORVAL

Torval was the greatest war-chief the northern clans produced in living memory — and in the oral tradition, living memory stretches back six generations, so this is saying something.

He was called Red-Hand not, as outsiders assume, because of blood, but because of an accident in his youth when he fell into a vat of berries during a harvest. The stain never fully washed out. He wore it without embarrassment. By the time he was war-chief, no one thought to laugh.

He united the eleven clans of the middle Varak range through a method that was unusual for the north: he talked to people. Not to their war-chiefs, not to the clan councils — to the ordinary people. The farmers, the herders, the women who did the actual work of keeping the clans fed. He asked what they needed. He wrote it down (he was one of the few northern war-chiefs who could write, having been taught by a border-town trader). Then he proposed to the war-chiefs that the confederation could provide these things if the clans stopped raiding each other and focused their energies outward.

The war-chiefs laughed at him. Six of the eleven agreed anyway.

Within a year of the confederation's founding, it had more food, more trade goods, and less internal violence than any comparable period in northern memory. The five remaining clans joined over the following five years, one by one, as the advantages became impossible to ignore.

At his death, Red-Hand Torval was asked if he had any regrets.

"Three," he said. "I should have started younger. I should have talked to the women first instead of the chiefs. And I should have kept better track of the berries incident."

THE BEAR WARRIOR OATH

(Recited upon initiation into the Bear Warrior order)

I swear this by the First Mother's warmth
and the cold that preceded her:

I will not run when my shield-brothers stand.
I will not sleep when the perimeter is unwatched.
I will not take more than my share from the common store.
I will not lie about my wounds to avoid the healer's tent.
I will not give my word and then reconsider.
I will not stand by when the weak are taken.

If I break these oaths,
let the cold take me.
If I keep them,
let the fire remember my name.

(It is considered poor form to name all the ways one might technically keep the letter of these oaths while violating their spirit. Every initiate thinks of several. The elders know they think of them. Neither party mentions it.)

THE TALE OF DAGRUN TWICE-DEAD

Dagrun was a hearth-singer of the Greymount clan who died of fever when she was forty-three, to the considerable grief of her clan, and then, three days later, got up.

The clan's reaction to this was mixed.

The healer, who had certified the death, was professionally embarrassed. The war-chief, who had already given away Dagrun's best cloak, was asked to return it. The rest of the clan was mostly just uncertain what the proper social protocol was for the situation.

Dagrun herself was calm about it. She said she had been somewhere else for three days, that it was quiet there, that she had seen people she had known who had died before her, and that none of them had anything particularly interesting to report — they were fine, they were resting, they missed the food.

She lived another thirty years after that. She said she was not afraid of dying because she had done it before and found it quite manageable.

When she finally died again, the clan left her body for three days before doing anything, just to be sure.

She did not get up.

But several clan members reported feeling, during those three days, that the air near her body was very slightly warmer than it should have been. The healer wrote this down in the clan records. The entry ends: "I do not know what to conclude from this. I am leaving it here for someone more qualified."

THE HEARTH SONG OF THE VARAK

(Sung at the start of winter, when the fires are first built for the season)

Fire, we know you.
You are the reason we survived last year.
We are asking you to help us survive this one.

We bring you: wood we cut with our own hands.
We bring you: fat from the animals we raised.
We bring you: the breath of everyone sitting here.

In return we ask:
warmth for the children.
Light for the healers.
Courage for the ones who must go out anyway.

Fire, we know you.
You are not kind.
You will burn a house as easily as a hearth.
We do not ask you to be kind.
We ask you to stay.

Stay until the spring.

THE CLASSIFICATION OF ENEMIES

(A practical taxonomy used in clan war-councils, as explained by War-Chief Brunda of the Ironfoot clan)

There are four kinds of enemy:

The enemy who wants something you have: negotiate first. Usually cheaper than fighting. Sometimes impossible, in which case, fight.

The enemy who is afraid of you: reassure them or make them more afraid, depending on which is more convenient. Do not ignore them. An afraid enemy who is also desperate is the most dangerous kind.

The enemy who hates you for old reasons: the hardest kind. You cannot negotiate with old hate. You can only wait it out, outlast it, try not to add new grievances, and hope that their grandchildren and your grandchildren find each other less offensive.

The enemy who simply wants to fight: these are the easiest and the worst. Easiest, because there is nothing to negotiate — you simply fight and see who wins. Worst, because winning teaches them nothing and they come back.

The war-chief added: "And then there is the enemy who used to be your friend, which is all four of the above at once. The gods love this one. We do not."

CLOSING SONG: FOR THE ONES WHO DID NOT COME BACK

Your name is in the fire.
We put it there.
Every year we will put it there again.

You are not forgotten.
That is what we can give you.
It is not enough.
Nothing is enough.
But it is what we have.

Your name is in the fire.
Say it with us.

— Dmitrios Ouroumenos, Border Scholar
   Written at the Greymount winter camp, third year of his residence
