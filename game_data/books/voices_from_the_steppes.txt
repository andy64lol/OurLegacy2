VOICES FROM THE STEPPES
A Collection of Kalovar Oral Poems, Songs, and Tales
Gathered and Translated by Travelling Scholar Erasimos of the Byzantra Academy
With Notes on Language, Custom, and the Difficulty of Translation

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

COLLECTOR'S PREFACE

These texts were gathered over eleven years of travel across the grasslands north of Yixuan, into the foothills of the Varak Mountains, and through the trading camps along the Durath River. Most were given to me by wandering poets — the Kalovar call them the zevari, the word meaning roughly "those who carry the fire." They are not priests or scholars but something older: living archives of a culture that had no writing until recently.

I am aware that some of what follows will be uncomfortable reading for citizens of Byzantra. The Kalovars are the people who destroyed our empire. I ask the reader to set that aside for the duration of this book. An enemy's songs are worth understanding. They explain, sometimes, what no military history can.

THE BIRTH SONG OF THE STEPPE

(Sung by Ayla Darkwind, zevari of the Northern Reaches, at the camp of the Vethrak clan)

Before the sky had edges
before the earth had names
the grass was already there.

The grass was already there and the wind moved through it
and in the moving was the first song
and in the first song was us.

We did not build our home.
We are the home.
The steppe is not a place we live.
The steppe is what we are
when we are most ourselves.

A man who sleeps in a stone city
does not dream of stars.
We pity him.
We do not say so.
But we pity him.

THE TALE OF KARA AND THE THREE RIVERS

(As told by elder Temür of the Batuk clan)

In the old time before the Törü was written down, there was a young rider named Kara who had nothing — no horse of his own, no clan, no family living. He had only a copper knife and the shirt on his back and the stubbornness, it is said, of ten ordinary men.

Kara came to the first river and the river-spirit spoke to him. "Cross me," said the spirit, "and I will take something from you." Kara said, "I have nothing to take." The spirit laughed. "Then you may pass for free." And Kara crossed.

He came to the second river and the spirit said the same. Kara still had nothing. Again he passed.

He came to the third river. The spirit was very old and very slow. It looked at Kara for a long time. "The other rivers said you have nothing," said the spirit. "But I see differently. I see a man who has survived two rivers. That is worth something." And it asked for his copper knife.

Kara gave the knife without complaint.

"Now you truly have nothing," said the spirit. "But you may cross. And when you reach the far shore you will find what you need."

On the far shore, there was a horse — an enormous grey stallion, lame in one leg. Kara did not complain. He nursed the horse's leg through a cold winter, eating almost nothing himself. By spring the horse could run. It ran like water runs — everywhere, effortlessly, as though it had never been lame.

That horse was the ancestor of the great Kalovar warhorses. And Kara — history does not remember what became of him, but the horses remember.

(The elder paused here and looked into the fire for a while before continuing.)

THE LAMENT OF THE CONQUERED CITY

(Heard from an unnamed zevari woman in the market of Kostantora)

Do not ask me to feel regret
for what my people won.

But some nights
in this city of stone and sorrow
I hear something in the alleys —
not a ghost, nothing so clear as that.
A quality of silence
that used to belong to other voices.

The libraries burned for three days.
I watched.
I wish I had not watched.

My grandfather told me:
conquer without cruelty when you can.
The world remembers cruelty longer than it remembers victories.

I think of him when I walk past the ruins of the Academy.
I do not know whose fault it is
that I feel two things at once.

The zevari say: hold all truths.
Even the ones that cut against each other.
Especially those.

THE LAW OF THE TÖRÜ

(From the formal recitation performed at the gathering of clan elders)

These are the bones of the law
that hold the people together:

Do not take the horse of a clan-brother.
Do not take the wife of a clan-brother.
Do not take the fire of a clan-brother on a cold night.
In all other matters, take what you can defend.

A promise made in daylight holds in darkness.
A promise made under duress is the duress's promise, not yours.

The elder speaks first. The young speak second. All speak once.
After that, the clan chief decides.

Blood debt is real. It must be paid.
But the clan council may commute blood for gold
if both families agree.
They usually agree, given time.

The steppe belongs to everyone.
The water belongs to everyone.
The grass belongs to everyone.
Your horse belongs to you.
Your fire belongs to you.
Your children belong to themselves, when they are old enough to ride.

These things were true before the khans.
These things will be true after the empires.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

CLOSING NOTE FROM THE COLLECTOR

I stayed three months in Kostantora and another two in the camps beyond the Durath. In that time I was robbed twice, saved from bandits once by a Kalovar warrior who wanted nothing in return, and treated to the finest hospitality I have ever experienced at a camp where every family was visibly hungry.

I return home having understood nothing I thought I understood, and having learned something I cannot name.

Perhaps that is what travel is for.

— Erasimos, Scholar
   Dictated in Nikomedeia, the year after the fall
