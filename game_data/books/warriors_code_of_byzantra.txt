THE WARRIOR'S CODE OF BYZANTRA
Being a Manual of Arms, Conduct, and Military Philosophy
Written by General Andronikos Doukas, Commander of the Eastern Legions
Composed in the Forty-Third Year of the Reign of Basileus Konstantinos VIII

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

FOREWORD

A soldier who does not understand why he fights is a sword with no hand to hold it. He may still cut — but he will not cut true.

I have commanded men for thirty-one years. I have buried more than I can count. I have seen armies of fifty thousand break before a force of eight — and a garrison of two hundred hold a mountain pass for six days against an empire. The difference, in every case, was not iron. It was something in the chest. Something that either burns there or does not.

This book is my attempt to describe that fire. What it is. How it is kindled. And how it is kept alive in the long cold between battles.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART ONE: THE SOLDIER'S BODY

Before a man can be a soldier, he must know his instrument.

The body is not separate from the will. A man who does not sleep enough will make slow decisions. A man who does not eat will not last through a long engagement. A man who overtrains will break at the worst moment. These are not inconveniences. They are tactical facts.

The legions of Byzantra eat twice a day — morning and evening. The morning meal is light: bread, oil, dried fruit if it can be spared. The evening meal is the soldier's reward — warm food, shared with one's unit, eaten without armour. I have found that the act of eating together does more for unit cohesion than any speech I have ever given.

Sleep is a weapon. Deprive your soldiers of it long enough and you will have an army of frightened, unreliable men. Deprive your enemy of it and you have already won half the battle. I have ordered night marches not to arrive early, but to ensure the enemy could hear our drums all night.

The sword arm is obvious. Less obvious is the shield arm — which takes more punishment and more discipline to maintain. In the academies, cadets spend as much time on footwork and shield work as on bladecraft. This is correct.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART TWO: THE SOLDIER'S MIND

Courage is not the absence of fear. Every man who tells you he feels no fear in battle is either lying or has never been in a real one.

True courage is performing your function despite the fear. This is what training is for — not to make a man fearless, but to make his reflexes faster than his terror. When the line breaks and men around you are dying, there is no time to think. You act from the body's memory. This is why we drill.

What thinking there is must happen before the battle. The good general thinks through every likely development — the enemy flanking manoeuvre, the supply line failure, the weather turning — and has already decided what he will do. By the time the crisis arrives, the decision is not a decision at all. It is the execution of a plan already made.

This principle applies to the common soldier as much as the commander. A soldier who knows his orders, knows his ground, and knows his comrades can function when everything else has collapsed. A soldier who does not know these things is lost the moment his centurion falls.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART THREE: THE SOLDIER'S HONOUR

There are armies in this world that permit atrocity. I have commanded against several. They are feared. They are also, invariably, eventually defeated — not always by better soldiers, but by the weight of the enemies their cruelty creates.

The legions of Byzantra do not sack cities that surrender. We do not execute prisoners who lay down their arms. We do not harm non-combatants except when military necessity — real military necessity, not the appetite of looters — demands it.

This is not weakness. This is strategy.

A city that knows it may surrender and be treated fairly will consider surrender. A city that expects massacre will fight to the last child. I have taken walled towns in three days that would have taken three months if we were known to be savages. The mathematics are not complicated.

Beyond strategy, there is something else. We are soldiers of Byzantra. The empire stands for something — law, learning, civilization, the idea that human beings can build something better than the ash and blood of the steppes. If we abandon what we stand for in the act of defending it, we have lost before the siege is done.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART FOUR: ON DEFEAT

Every general loses a battle. The ones who matter are those who learn from it.

A defeat is information. The enemy exploited a weakness you did not know you had, or used a tactic you had not anticipated, or simply outfought your men at a point where it mattered. Understand which of these it was. Fix what can be fixed. Accept what cannot.

What you cannot do — what no commander of mine ever did twice — is blame the soldiers for a defeat that was a failure of command. The men fight where you put them and how you trained them. If they broke, ask first what you did wrong.

My own worst defeat — at the Vethros River crossing, thirty years ago — was entirely my fault. I rushed a crossing I should have scouted. The enemy was waiting. I lost four hundred men in twenty minutes. I have thought about those four hundred men most days since.

They are why I wrote this book.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

CLOSING WORDS

I am old now. My sword arm aches in the winter and I have lost count of the campaigns I have ridden.

If I could say one thing to a young soldier beginning his service: learn your trade with everything you have, but never forget that the man standing opposite you on the field also has a mother, a home, a reason he believes he is fighting. This does not make him your friend. It makes him human. And wars between humans, unlike wars between armies, have endings.

Fight to win. Know why you are fighting. Come home if you can.

— General Andronikos Doukas
   The Citadel of Nikomedeia, Eastern Byzantra
