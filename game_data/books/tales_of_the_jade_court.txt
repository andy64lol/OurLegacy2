TALES OF THE JADE COURT
Stories, Parables, and Histories from the Eastern Kingdoms
Gathered and Translated by Scholar-Merchant Alexandros Katakalon
With Assistance from Madam Wei Linhua, Court Storyteller of the Sunlit Principality of Jinshin

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

TRANSLATOR'S NOTE

The stories in this collection were told to me over three years in the court of the Sunlit Prince of Jinshin. Some are ancient parables. Some are accounts of real events. Some — and Madam Wei was honest about this — are somewhere in between, because in the eastern tradition, a story's truth is not measured by whether it literally happened but by what it teaches.

I have tried to translate faithfully. Where I have failed, the fault is mine. Where the stories are strange to western sensibility, the strangeness is intentional. These are not our stories, and that is precisely why they are worth reading.

THE STORY OF THE JADE MINISTER

In the reign of the seventh Sunlit Prince, there was a minister named Chen Bao who had risen from the rice paddies of the inner provinces to the jade-pillared halls of the court. He was the cleverest man in the palace — everyone said so, including Chen Bao himself, which was his first mistake.

The Prince summoned his ministers and said: "My treasury is empty. My armies need feeding. My people are hungry. I need a solution by morning."

All the ministers spoke at once, filling the room with proposals — new taxes, border raids, loans from the merchant guilds. Chen Bao said nothing.

When the others had finished, the Prince looked at him. "You have not spoken, Chen Bao."

"I was listening," said Chen Bao. "All the proposals will work for one season and create two problems in the next. The treasury is empty because the examination system has been corrupted — minor officials are selling examination places to rich families' sons, which means the capable men who would fill the positions well are farming rice instead. Fix the corruption, and within five years you will have officials who know how to fill the treasury themselves."

The Prince said: "And in the meantime?"

Chen Bao said: "In the meantime, you must borrow. But borrow from the merchant guilds at terms that give them trade rights, not at terms that give them political power. There is a difference."

The Prince implemented Chen Bao's plan. It worked. Chen Bao was given a title and a house in the palace district and a hat with a peacock feather.

The following year, Chen Bao used his new position to place his nephew in a promising examination position without going through the examination. This was discovered. His title, house, and peacock-feathered hat were removed.

When asked if he regretted it, Chen Bao said: "I regret being caught."

This is considered a cautionary tale. About what, exactly, scholars continue to debate.

THE SILKWORM EMPRESS

Before the great secret of silk was known, the court of the first Emperor wore rough linen like everyone else.

The woman who discovered silk is called in all accounts simply the Silkworm Empress, though she was not an empress when she discovered it — she was an imperial consort, one of hundreds, spending her days in a garden that the historians agree was very beautiful and very boring.

One afternoon, she was drinking tea beneath a mulberry tree when a silkworm cocoon fell from a branch into her cup. Attempting to remove it, she found the hot tea had begun to loosen the fibre, and as she pulled it, a single continuous thread emerged — fine, strong, and lustrous in the afternoon light.

She spent the next seven years in that garden, not bored at all.

By the time she emerged, she had developed the complete method: cultivating silkworms, feeding them mulberry leaves, harvesting the cocoons at precisely the right moment, reeling the thread, and weaving it on a loom she had designed herself.

The Emperor was astonished. He elevated her immediately to First Empress. He also, prudently, made the method a state secret, punishable by death to share with foreigners.

She is still honoured in the eastern kingdoms. Her feast day is celebrated with the wearing of silk and the drinking of tea into which, by custom, you briefly dip something small before removing it.

The western merchants who have tried to steal the silkworm method have never understood why they keep failing. The reason is that the secret is not kept in one place. Every person in the silk industry knows one part of the process completely and the rest only partially. There is no single person to bribe or kidnap who knows it all.

The Silkworm Empress thought of that too.

THE GENERAL AND THE GARDENER

In the time of the border wars, there was a general named Wei Chengling who had won eleven battles in fifteen years and was accordingly given command of the entire eastern army.

Upon receiving this honour, he immediately resigned from the army.

The Emperor summoned him. "You have been given the highest military honour in the empire. Why do you leave?"

Wei Chengling said: "Because now I must sit in a tent and move wooden blocks on a map while other men do the fighting. I am a general because I am good at fighting. I am not good at moving wooden blocks. Give the command to Minister Huang, who is excellent at moving blocks. I will fight under him."

This was considered scandalous. A senior general serving under a minister? But the Emperor, who was pragmatic, agreed.

The arrangement worked. Wei Chengling won three more battles. Minister Huang kept the supply lines running and the Emperor informed. The border was secured.

Wei Chengling retired at sixty to grow chrysanthemums. He was better at it than anyone expected.

The moral, as Madam Wei explained it to me: "Know what you are for. The great mistake is assuming you are good at everything because you are good at one thing."

THE GHOST RIVER

(A tale from the inner provinces, told to frighten children and, apparently, to occasionally frighten adults)

There is a stretch of the Yi Xuan River, three days south of the inner province capital, where the water runs black for half a mile. The locals call it the Ghost River.

The explanation they give is historical: eight hundred years ago, a great battle was fought at this crossing. An army of the northern mountain clans against the forces of the then-Emperor. It lasted three days. Neither side won decisively; both sides lost so many men that the water ran red.

The water does not run red now. It runs black, which the locals say is worse — red is blood and blood washes away. Black is the colour of what is left when the blood has been and gone.

There are things seen in that stretch of river by people who have reason to cross it at night. Shadows under the water. Lights that move. Voices that speak in accents no one has heard in living memory.

The fishermen will not fish that stretch.
The boatmen will not linger there.
A temple sits on the western bank where monks burn paper offerings every evening at sunset.

I crossed it twice. Once in daylight. Once at night, when the boat had no choice.

I will not describe what I saw at night. I am a scholar. I deal in evidence. I will say only that I lit incense at the temple on the eastern bank the following morning and the monks did not find this strange.

THE ART OF THE EXAMINATION

The great examination system is now eight centuries old. Its purpose was to replace aristocratic inheritance with talent: to staff the empire's civil service with the most capable men regardless of their birth.

Whether it achieves this is debated endlessly in the teahouses of River's Crown.

What is certain: the examinations are extraordinarily difficult. Candidates spend years — sometimes their entire youth — memorising texts, practicing calligraphy, and studying history and philosophy. The highest examinations last three days and are conducted in individual stone cells to prevent cheating.

The highest passing rate in the history of the system was four percent.

Most men who sit for the examinations fail. Many sit multiple times across decades. There are famous cases of men who sat for thirty years before passing. There are slightly more famous cases of men who sat for thirty years and never passed.

What does a society do with the enormous number of very educated, very unsuccessful examination candidates? This is a question the Jade Court prefers not to discuss. The answers range from "they become teachers" to "they become revolutionaries," depending on the decade.

— Alexandros Katakalon
   Golden Harbour, Year Four of his Eastern Residence
