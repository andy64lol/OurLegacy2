THE MERCHANT'S ROAD
A Practical Traveller's Guide to the Trade Routes of the Known World
By Niketas the Far-Wanderer, formerly of Constanplea, now of Everywhere

Fifth Edition — Revised, Corrected, and Extensively Annotated Following Recent Political Developments

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

A NOTE ON THIS EDITION

Much has changed since the fourth edition. Constanplea no longer exists in the form described in those pages. The great market districts of the capital are now operating under Kalovar administration and have been renamed. The western trade routes through the Thessaric Pass are now controlled by the Merchant Brotherhood of Varak, who charge a higher toll than the imperial authorities did but maintain the road considerably better. Several cities I described as safe are now unsafe, and at least one I described as dangerous has become, inexplicably, a pleasant place to stop for lunch.

I have tried to update everything. I am sure I have missed things. This is the nature of guidebooks: the world moves faster than the ink dries.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART ONE: THE GREAT ROUTES

THE INNER SEA COASTAL ROAD

This is the oldest trade route in the known world, running along the northern coast of the Inner Sea from the Yixuan territories in the east to the western kingdoms at the far end. It is well-maintained, heavily policed (under Kalovar administration in the eastern half), and generally safe for a merchant travelling with at least two armed companions.

Advantages: reliable, well-supplied, many inns.
Disadvantages: expensive tolls at every major crossing, heavy competition from other merchants, dull scenery.
Travel time: Approximately forty days from east to west, depending on weather and delays at the checkpoints.
Notable hazard: The stretch between Korindra and the Varath headlands is controlled by the Blackwater Guild, who are technically pirates but in practice function as an unofficial toll authority. Pay their fee. It is not negotiable, they are not bluffing, and they outnumber you.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE NORTHERN MOUNTAIN PASSES

There are three passes through the Varak Mountains. I have used all three. My recommendation, having survived all three, is to use the Southgate Pass in summer and autumn, avoid all three in winter, and use whichever one has the fewest bandits in spring (this varies year to year; ask locally).

The Highwatch Pass offers the fastest route but is controlled by the Greycap Brotherhood, who will demand payment, search your goods, and confiscate anything they find interesting. Do not carry anything interesting.

The Southgate Pass is longer but safer and offers better views. There is an excellent inn at the midpoint called the Stone Hearth. Tell them Niketas sent you. They will not give you a discount, but they will know I am still alive, which I believe they have begun to doubt.

The Coldridge Pass should not be used. I am including it only so you know not to use it.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE STEPPE ROAD

Since the rise of the Kalovanim Empire, a new route has opened: straight across the northern steppes, following the Kalovar courier roads. This is a remarkable development. Twenty years ago, crossing the steppe meant gambling your life against weather, bandits, and clan politics. Today the courier road is maintained, the waystation inns are adequate, and Kalovar law (harshly enforced) protects merchants in transit.

Advantages: shortest route between east and west, no sea-sickness.
Disadvantages: cold beyond belief for six months of the year, very little water, the waystations serve a local dish called kerat that I find difficult to describe and harder to forget.
Notable hazard: do not ride off the road. The road is protected. Off the road is not the road.

PART TWO: CITY BY CITY

KOSTANTORA (formerly Constanplea)

What to buy: silk, metalwork, spices from the eastern trade, Kalovar leatherwork (underrated, very durable), books (the libraries were damaged but not destroyed; sellers operate in the old Scholar's Quarter).

What to sell: anything from the western kingdoms, particularly glass, refined spirits, and clockwork mechanisms, which the Kalovars find fascinating.

What to avoid: discussing politics. Discussing religion. Discussing the previous name of the city. Looking sad in the vicinity of the old imperial buildings. Looking happy in the vicinity of the old imperial buildings.

Currency: the Kalovar tul is now the official currency, but imperial gold is accepted everywhere, usually at a slight premium for the metal.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

NIKOMEDEIA

Still operating as a free city, though for how long is a subject of considerable discussion among its citizens.

What to buy: excellent wine, olive oil, the best paper in the known world (made by the Papadopoulos family in the merchant district; buy the heavy grade).
What to sell: anything you bought cheaply elsewhere.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

YIXUAN (the Eastern City-States)

The Yixuan do not call themselves Yixuan — that is what western merchants call them. They have a name for themselves in their own language that I am told translates roughly as "the people of the long river." I call them Yixuan in this book because that is what the maps say and arguing with maps is exhausting.

What to buy: silk (obviously), lacquerwork, the red ceramic tea bowls which sell for remarkable prices in the west, medicines and herbs unavailable elsewhere.
What to sell: iron goods, amber, furs, Byzantran glass (now rare and therefore more valuable than it was).
What to avoid: the third district of the lower city after dark. I cannot say more than this without being impolite to people who have otherwise treated me generously.

PART THREE: GENERAL ADVICE

ON LANGUAGES

You will need: the common trade tongue (you already have this, presumably), some Kalovar (a little goes a long way; learn the words for "merchant," "toll," "I am not a spy," and "please"), and ideally Yixuan script for eastern markets. I recommend hiring a translator in Kostantora rather than attempting the eastern languages yourself — Yixuan has four tones and I spent two weeks accidentally ordering things I did not want because I could not hear the difference.

ON MONEY

Carry gold. Not too much gold. Enough gold to look credible and not enough to be worth killing for. This is a balance that experience will teach you better than I can describe.

Carry some bronze for ordinary purchases. In many markets, arriving with gold for a small transaction marks you as either very rich or very careless, and attracts attention either way.

ON SAFETY

Travel with companions. The road is safer than it was twenty years ago, but it is not safe. Two armed companions is the minimum for any route outside a major city. Four is comfortable. More than six and you start to look like a military force, which creates its own problems.

Know where the waystations are before you leave. Know the local disputes. Ask everyone you meet what they have heard. Rumour on the road is often wrong, but wrong rumour can still save your life if it makes you cautious at the right moment.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

CLOSING WORDS

I have been on the road for thirty-one years. I have made money, lost money, made it again. I have eaten things I cannot identify in places that did not appear on any map. I have been robbed three times and swindled more times than I can count. I have also been given shelter by strangers, guided safely through dangerous places by people who owed me nothing, and fed warm meals when I was cold and lost.

The world is large and mostly indifferent to any individual in it. But it is not cruel, except in patches. And the patches move.

Keep moving. Keep your eyes open. Pay your tolls.

— Niketas, the Far-Wanderer
