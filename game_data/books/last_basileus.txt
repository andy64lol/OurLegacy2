THE LAST BASILEUS: A LIFE OF KONSTANTINOS IX
Emperor of Byzantra, Defender of the Eternal Flame, Last of His Line
Written by Lady Eudokia Asanina, Court Historian and Member of the Imperial Academy
Composed in Exile, Three Years After the Fall

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

FOR THE READER

I am not a soldier. I am not a general. I am a historian who happened to be in the palace the night the walls fell and who managed, by a combination of luck and unflattering disguise, to escape the city before the morning.

What I write here is not an account of the siege — that exists in Theophanes' more celebrated work. This is an account of a man. The last man to hold an impossible office and the only one in a generation who held it with anything resembling dignity.

I knew Konstantinos IX for eleven years. I served in his court, attended his councils, and sat across from him at dinner on more occasions than I can count. I did not always agree with him. He was not a simple man to agree with. He was also not a simple man.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART ONE: THE PRINCE

He was not supposed to be emperor. He was the third son. The throne was meant to pass to his eldest brother, Andronikos, who died of a fever at twenty-three. Then to his middle brother, Ioannis, who was murdered in one of the palace conspiracies that the court produced with relentless regularity. And so the crown came to Konstantinos, the youngest, the quiet one, who had spent his youth studying philosophy and history while his brothers played at politics.

He came to the throne at thirty-one. The empire had been shrinking for a century. The treasury was in a condition that the senior accountant described to me, in a whispered aside, as "conceptually empty." The great families were fighting each other with considerably more energy than they were fighting the Kalovars. Two of the frontier provinces had simply stopped paying taxes and were waiting to see what would happen.

What happened was Konstantinos.

He spent his first three years doing things emperors did not usually do: attending to administration, sitting in on court cases, reviewing the accounts personally. He dismissed three treasury officials for corruption, which caused a scandal that lasted for months and resulted in a much cleaner treasury. He negotiated, rather than fought, with the frontier provinces — returning certain privileges in exchange for the resumption of tax payments. He was mocked for this by the war party. The provinces paid their taxes.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART TWO: THE EMPEROR

He ruled for fourteen years before the siege.

They were not great years in the way of conquest or triumph. The empire did not grow. In a few places it shrank, losing territories that had been disputed for decades to Kalovar pressure. Konstantinos chose not to fight battles he calculated he would lose, which the war faction called cowardice and I called arithmetic.

But the empire was more stable in his middle reign than it had been in the previous fifty years. The great families were not friends — they were never friends — but they were, for a time, managed. The trade routes recovered somewhat. The Academy received more funding than it had seen in a generation.

He married late — at forty-two, a political match to a princess of the western kingdoms, a kind woman named Theodora who bore him no children and died of illness seven years before the siege. He did not remarry. He spent the evenings after his wife's death reading, alone, in the imperial library. The servants reported that he sometimes did not eat.

I do not know what he felt. He rarely spoke of it. He was not a man who spoke of what he felt.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART THREE: THE SIEGE

Sultan Velkar's army arrived in early spring. The scouts had known it was coming for months. The diplomats had sent letters. The western kingdoms had been asked for help. Some sent sympathy. None sent soldiers.

Konstantinos made his decision quietly and without apparent anguish, which I believe was not the absence of anguish but simply its containment. He sent away the members of the court who could be sent away. He ordered the treasury valuables distributed to trusted hands throughout the city. He gave those who wished to leave permission to leave.

He himself did not leave.

I asked him, three days before the final assault, whether he had considered it. He looked at me for a moment with an expression I have thought about ever since — something between amusement and exhaustion. "Of course," he said. "I considered it the way one considers many things. Then I stopped considering it."

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE LAST DAYS

He rode the walls every night. I went with him twice. The city below was quiet — the quiet of people who knew and could not unsee what they knew. Campfires of the Kalovar host beyond the walls stretched in every direction.

He spoke to the soldiers. Not speeches — he did not favour speeches. He spoke to individuals: asked their names, asked where they were from, asked if they needed anything. He remembered the names when he saw them again. Soldiers notice this.

On the last morning, before dawn, he dressed himself in full imperial armour — purple cloak, gilded breastplate, the crown that had sat in a cupboard for the duration of the siege. He rode his horse slowly along the inner face of the wall. Those who saw him said he looked neither frightened nor resigned.

He looked, they said, like a man who had decided.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

WHAT REMAINED

His body was identified by a soldier of the Kalovar army — a young officer named Ervan, who deserves credit for ensuring it was treated with respect. Sultan Velkar, to his own credit, ordered a burial appropriate to an emperor. Whatever else may be said of him, that act was honourable.

The empire ended there, in the rubble of the western gate, on a morning in early summer.

But I think — and this is not the historian speaking, this is simply me — that it had also ended in a sense much earlier, and that what Konstantinos did, in refusing to leave, was choose not to let the ending be shameful. He could not save the empire. He could only choose how it ended.

He chose well.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

LADY EUDOKIA'S CLOSING NOTE

I am old now. I live in a small city in the western kingdoms that is kind to exiles and has a decent library. I think about Byzantra every day. I think about what we built and lost. I think about the people who did not escape.

Mostly I think about a man reading alone in a library after dinner, who had already done the arithmetic and knew what was coming and read his books anyway.

I hope, wherever he is, there is a good library.

— Lady Eudokia Asanina
   Written in the western city of Veranthos, Year Three of the Exile
