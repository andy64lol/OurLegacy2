THE EASTERN ALCHEMIST'S COMPENDIUM
Medicines, Preparations, and Philosophical Principles of Yixuan Alchemy
Translated from the Yixuan by Scholar-Physician Kallikrates of the Byzantra Academy
With Original Commentary by Master Herbalist Shén Wúxiǎo of River's Crown

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PREFACE BY THE TRANSLATOR

The discipline the eastern practitioners call — and I use their term loosely here — "the art of transformation" is not alchemy as we understand it in the west. Where our alchemists seek to transmute base metals into gold or find the philosopher's stone that will extend life indefinitely, the eastern practitioners are concerned with something both more and less ambitious: understanding the forces that move through living things, and learning to redirect them.

They do not believe in transmutation of metals. They believe in the transformation of the body's own energies.

Whether this is correct is a question I cannot answer. What I can say is that several of their medicines work, and work better than equivalent western preparations, and that their explanations for why they work — though incompatible with our theoretical framework — produce results that our theories often do not.

I have translated as faithfully as possible. Where Master Shén's original text assumes knowledge of concepts that have no western equivalent, I have added explanatory notes in brackets.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

CHAPTER ONE: THE FOUNDATIONS

Master Shén writes:

All living things contain the vital current [Editor's note: the original term is lìqì, which the master translates for foreign students as "the breath in the blood"]. This current moves through the body along invisible pathways that the old masters mapped over centuries of observation. When the current flows freely, the body is well. When it is blocked, stagnant, or excessive, illness follows.

The alchemist's task — which I would prefer we call the healer-philosopher's task, as "alchemist" carries unfortunate associations with charlatans — is to assist the body's own current in flowing correctly.

We do not heal. The body heals. We remove obstructions.

[Translator's note: I found this principle baffling until I watched Master Shén treat a patient with a high fever by applying compresses of vinegar and willow bark, which brought the fever down within two hours. When I asked how this worked within his framework, he explained that the fever was the body's current fighting an intrusion; the treatment helped the fight without overwhelming the fighter. I found this explanation puzzling and the result remarkable.]

CHAPTER TWO: THE FIVE ELEMENTS IN MEDICINE

The eastern system divides the world's forces into five elements: Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, and Water. These are not literal wood and fire — they are qualities, tendencies, relationships.

Wood is growth, flexibility, the force of spring. Fire is warmth, transformation, the force of summer. Earth is stability, nourishment, the center. Metal is clarity, precision, the force of autumn. Water is depth, stillness, the force of winter.

Every organ in the body corresponds to one of these elements. The heart corresponds to Fire. The lungs to Metal. The liver to Wood. The kidney to Water. The stomach to Earth.

When an organ is troubled, the healer looks at the relationships between elements — which is the source, which is drawing too much, which is not giving enough — and adjusts with food, herbs, movement, or the placement of fine needles at specific points on the body's pathways.

[Translator's note: The needle treatment caused me considerable skepticism until I was present when Master Shén treated a patient with chronic headaches that had resisted western medicine. Four sessions of needle treatment, applied to points on the feet and hands that seemed entirely unrelated to the head, produced a complete resolution of the condition. I have no theoretical framework for why this occurred.]

CHAPTER THREE: THE GREAT HERBAL

The following are preparations in common use among eastern practitioners. I have included only those for which I was able to observe consistent results and obtain enough of the base materials to carry samples back for western evaluation.

GINSENG ROOT (the eastern preparation)
Dried and simmered in water for two hours, the resulting tea is prescribed for exhaustion, weakness after illness, and impairment of the body's current during recovery. Several other functions are claimed. The taste is bitter-sweet. I took it every morning for three months and found myself, perhaps coincidentally, more alert.

ARTEMISIA (Mugwort)
Used both dried and fresh. Applied as a warm compress, it relieves cold-type pain (joint aches, cold extremities). Burned as a smouldering cone near specific pathway points, it is used to stimulate sluggish organs. The western pharmacopeia knows artemisia but does not use it this way. The results I observed suggest we may be missing something.

THE GOLDEN FLOWER PREPARATION
A compound of twelve herbs that Master Shén declined to specify completely, as it is considered a professional secret. The preparation is used for severe fevers and has a documented record of efficacy that even conservative western physicians who have examined the case records find difficult to dismiss.

JADE SPRING TEA
Not, despite the name, made from jade. It contains chrysanthemum, wolfberry, and several other components. It is taken daily for eye health and what Master Shén calls "calming of the upper current." It tastes quite pleasant. I brought back a significant quantity.

CHAPTER FOUR: ON THE LONG-LIFE PREPARATIONS

Master Shén asked me to note, and notes himself in the original, that the eastern tradition does not promise immortality. Several historical practitioners claimed to be pursuing such a thing. Several of them died, often of poisoning from the heavy metals they were incorporating into their immortality preparations. This is considered a cautionary tale in the eastern tradition as well.

What the long-life preparations actually seek is not immortality but vitality — a long life lived with good health and clear mind. The interventions are dietary, exercise-based, and herbal. Some are philosophical: the cultivation of equanimity, acceptance of change, maintenance of meaningful relationships.

Master Shén, who is eighty-three years old, climbs the stairs to his study every morning without assistance. He attributes this to good vegetables, adequate sleep, and not worrying about things outside his control.

Western philosophy has said similar things. We have been saying them for centuries. We tend not to act on them.

MASTER SHÉN'S CLOSING WORDS (TRANSLATED)

The western visitor always wants to know the secret. There is no secret. There is only paying attention.

Pay attention to the body. It tells you what it needs before it becomes ill; you must listen before the crisis, not after.

Pay attention to the seasons. We are not separate from the world we live in. When the world turns cold and inward, do not pretend it is still summer.

Pay attention to the small things. A slight change in energy, in sleep, in appetite — these are the body's first words. Wait until it is shouting and you have less time.

The greatest medicine I know is a quiet mind and enough sleep.

The second greatest medicine is good vegetables.

After that, the rest of it helps.

— Master Shén Wúxiǎo, River's Crown
— Translated by Kallikrates, Byzantra Academy of Medicine
