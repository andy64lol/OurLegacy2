THE NEW ORDER: LIFE IN KOSTANTORA UNDER THE OSMANS
A Personal Account and Civic Observation
Written by Miriam of the Lower City, formerly of Constanplea
Date of Writing: Approximately Fifteen Years After the Fall

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

I am not a scholar. I want to be clear about that from the beginning. I have read some books — the scholars in the Academy taught those of us who wanted to learn, before the Academy became a barracks — but I am a woman who sells cloth at the market, who watches and listens, and who has lived in this city through its destruction and its reconstruction. What follows is what I have seen with my own eyes.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE CITY THAT WAS

Before the siege, Constanplea had perhaps half a million people. Many of them fled in the years before the fall, sensing what was coming. Many more were killed in the fighting or taken in the immediate aftermath. The city I grew up in — the city of neighbourhood festivals and church bells and market days in the great square — that city does not exist anymore.

What exists now is Kostantora.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE SLAVE MARKETS

I will speak plainly about this, because most accounts I have seen do not.

The slave markets at Port Kostantora are the most visible and most terrible feature of the new city. They operate openly, in permanent structures, with registered traders who pay taxes to the Sultan's treasury on every transaction. This is not hidden. The Osman authorities are not ashamed of it. Slavery has been part of their empire since before Constanplea fell, and the conquest of our city added immensely to the supply.

The people sold at Kostantora come from everywhere. Many are prisoners of war — captured soldiers and civilians from the empire's expanding borders. Many are people taken in raids on the Byzantran villages that still exist in the former empire's territory. Some are people sold by their own families in desperation, during the famines that followed the conquest. Some are debt slaves — a system the Osmans introduced — people who could not repay their creditors and whose bodies became the debt settlement.

I have friends who were taken. I do not know what became of them. The slave ships leave the harbour regularly, bound for estates and mines throughout the empire.

The Osman authorities will tell you that the institution is regulated. That there are rules. That masters face penalties for excessive cruelty. Perhaps this is true. A gilded cage is still a cage.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE FAITH AND ITS IMPOSITION

The Osmans have their own faith, brought from the desert lands where it was born. I have read enough of their sacred texts to understand it — it preaches submission to the divine will, charity, justice, the brotherhood of believers. In the hands of sincere people, it is a genuinely good faith.

In the hands of an empire using it as a tool of control, it is something else.

The conversion of Constanplea's churches was systematic. The Church of the Eternal Flame — our greatest cathedral, our most holy site — was converted within the year. The interior mosaics, centuries of devotional art, were whitewashed. New calligraphy was inscribed over them. The building still stands; in some ways it is still beautiful. But it is not ours anymore.

The Sultan's policy, at least officially, does not require the city's non-Muslim residents to convert. We are allowed to practice our own faith, pay an additional tax, and live in peace — more or less. I will not pretend the arrangement is comfortable. The additional tax is enough to impoverish many families. The better positions in commerce and administration are closed to non-believers. The pressure is not violent, most of the time. It does not need to be. It accumulates. Over time, each generation of children raised in this city knows a little less of the old faith and a little more of the new.

Conversion buys advancement. Resistance to conversion buys increasing poverty. The calculation is not difficult.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE TAKING OF CHILDREN

The system they call the devshirme — I have no word for it in our language, so I use theirs — is perhaps the most disquieting thing I have witnessed in fifteen years of living under Osman rule.

Every few years, officials from the Sultan's administration travel through the non-Muslim communities of the empire. They select boys — healthy ones, intelligent-looking ones, typically between the ages of eight and eighteen. These boys are taken from their families, converted to the Osman faith, educated in the Sultan's schools, and trained for the Janissary corps or the imperial administration.

They become, in effect, the Sultan's men. Many of them rise to positions of great power. The Grand Vizier himself, it is said, began as a devshirme child.

Their families receive a small payment. They receive no choice.

I have watched mothers learn that their sons are to be taken. I will not describe what that looks like. I do not have the words, and I would not inflict them on a reader if I did.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE CITY LIVES

I have written much darkness. Let me write something else, because honesty requires it.

Kostantora is alive. That is the truth that sits alongside all the other truths.

The markets are full. Ships crowd the harbour from a dozen nations. The great mosques the Sultan has built are genuinely beautiful. The roads are maintained. The poor are fed from public kitchens — a practice the Osmans brought with them and which, I will admit, Byzantra never managed at this scale.

Crime in the city is lower than it was in the chaotic years before the siege, when the empire was visibly collapsing and the desperate did what desperation demands. The new administration is harsh, but it is not arbitrary — there is a law, and the law is applied consistently, which is more than could be said for Byzantra's final decades.

The scholars of the Osman faith are, many of them, genuinely learned. The Sultan's library is growing. Knowledge is not despised here. It is valued, selectively.

Life continues. People are born and die. Children play in the streets. The market bells ring at dawn. Some mornings, for a moment, I can almost forget what was here before.

Then I remember the church bells that used to ring at dawn.

Then I remember.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

A NOTE FOR THOSE WHO FIND THIS ACCOUNT

I am writing this partly because I am afraid it will be forgotten. The Osman authorities do not censor — not exactly — but they do not preserve accounts like this one either. The libraries they maintain tell the story of the city from the moment of its liberation, as they call it. The story before that is left to fade.

Do not let it fade.

Remember Constanplea. Remember Byzantra. Remember what was here, and what it cost to take it away.

That is the least we can do for those who did not survive.

— Miriam of the Lower City, Kostantora
  Formerly of the Midmarket district, Constanplea
