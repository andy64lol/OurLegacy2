ORIGIA: A HISTORY OF THE FIRST SETTLEMENT
Being a Complete Account of the Village Whose Name the World Has Forgotten

By Chronicler Aldus Vren, Royal Surveyor, Third Grade
Commissioned by the Bureau of Historical Records
Dedicated to every innkeeper who has ever corrected a traveller and been ignored

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

A NOTE ON THE TITLE

This book is about a village called Origia.

You probably know it as Starting Village.

This is incorrect. The village has a name. It is Origia. It has been Origia for longer than most of the surrounding region has been inhabited, longer than the roads that pass through it were roads, longer than anyone now alive can remember. The name is carved into the foundation stone of the old mill. It is written in the land survey records dating to the second century of the Byzantra Empire. It appears in the Primordial Scriptures — listed second in the holy inventory of places, directly after Eldenmoor — in a list that scholars have spent centuries debating.

The village is called Origia.

Adventurers call it Starting Village. They have done so for approximately one hundred and forty years, and at this point it seems unlikely to stop.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART ONE: THE FOUNDING

Origia was established in what historians date to the early settlement period, when the first organised migrations moved inland from the coastal ports. The settlers who chose the site did so for sensible reasons: a freshwater spring, a sheltered valley, soil that actually produced crops without resentment.

The name they gave it comes from the old southern tongue. "Origia" is derived from the word for beginning, or origin, or first-thing, depending on which dialect you consult. The settlers, apparently, were not subtle people. They founded a village and named it "the place where things begin." This turned out to be prophetic in ways they could not have anticipated.

The founding families left no detailed records. What we know of them comes from the Bureau's land surveys, the Primordial Scriptures (which list the village but offer no biographical detail), and a carved wooden beam in the inn that reads: "Here stood the first house. It leaked." Scholars have agreed that this is probably true.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART TWO: ORIGIA IN THE EARLY IMPERIAL PERIOD

By the time the Byzantra Empire extended its administrative reach into the western lands, Origia was already what it would remain for centuries: a modest, functional, thoroughly unremarkable village.

The Imperial census of the fourth century lists Origia with a population of three hundred and twelve, seven functioning wells, one inn, one smithy, one shrine of no particular denomination, and — a census-taker's note appended in different ink — "an unusually high number of people who claim to be passing through."

This last observation is telling. Even then, Origia was a crossroads. The roads that passed through it connected the forest paths to the north, the ancient ruins to the east, and the wider world in every other direction. People arrived. People left. The people who arrived and stayed were, statistically speaking, fewer than average.

The inn, which stood on the eastern side of the village square, recorded its guests in a leather-bound ledger. The Bureau holds eighty-three of these ledgers in its archive. Reviewing them, a pattern emerges: almost no one recorded Origia as their destination. They were always on the way to somewhere else. Origia was where they slept before setting out.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART THREE: HOW A VILLAGE LOST ITS NAME

The precise moment when "Starting Village" entered common usage cannot be determined. The earliest written use of the phrase the Bureau has found dates to approximately one hundred and forty years ago, in a private letter from one Gavin of the Sword (an adventurer, presumably) to a friend: "After Starting Village I took the north road and the forest was worse than anyone warned."

Gavin of the Sword did not explain why he called it Starting Village. He probably assumed his friend would know what he meant. The friend, whoever they were, apparently did.

The phrase spread. Adventurers are, as a profession, prone to creating their own vocabulary, and "Starting Village" had an obvious logic to it: it was, for a great many of them, the village where they started. Heroes began there. Legends launched from its square. The road out of Origia led — through one path or another — to every notable location, every great danger, every treasure and catastrophe that the world had to offer.

The innkeeper of that era, a woman named Bessie Thorpe, reportedly spent twenty years insisting to every guest that the village was called Origia. Her daughter gave up on this project. Her granddaughter, the current innkeeper, no longer mentions it unless directly asked, at which point she says "Origia" in a tone that suggests she has explained this before and does not expect it to help.

She is correct that it does not help.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART FOUR: WHAT THE VILLAGE IS, ACTUALLY

The Bureau's current survey of Origia describes a village of standard construction: stone-and-timber buildings, a central well, an inn of reasonable quality, a smithy that produces functional if unspectacular equipment, and a small shrine maintained by Elder Mira, who has occupied her position so long that several younger residents assume she was born there holding a staff.

The roads out of Origia are, as noted, numerous. This is the village's defining characteristic. Go north and you reach the forest path, and beyond it the deeper wilderness. Go east and you find the ancient ruins, which draw scholars and treasure hunters in roughly equal measure. Go further and eventually you reach Eldenmoor City — whose High Archivist Pellan once wrote to the Bureau asking that we note, for the record, that Origia was named before Eldenmoor in the Primordial Scriptures, and that she considered this "a matter of historical importance that the Bureau has so far failed to address." The Bureau addressed it by filing her letter.

Ashvale lies to the south. Thornwick to the southwest. Both maintain what might be called formal relations with Origia — they acknowledge its existence, they trade with it occasionally, and they do not call it Starting Village, which Origia's residents appreciate more than they say aloud.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART FIVE: NOTABLE PERSONS OF ORIGIA

The Bureau's records contain the names of over forty individuals born or long-resident in Origia who went on to achieve some form of documented distinction. A partial list:

Blacksmith Torren's grandfather, Edvin, forged the blade used by the hero Cassia of the Three Campaigns. Cassia is widely celebrated. Edvin is listed in the Bureau's records as "Edvin, blade-maker, Origia."

Elder Mira has served as the village's spiritual counsel for longer than the Bureau has been tracking. She has declined, three times, to be interviewed for this record. Her exact age is unknown. The Bureau suspects she knows something we do not.

The adventurer known as "the Wandering Blade" — whose real name was Petra Vorn, which the legends have entirely dropped — was born in Origia, walked out of it at seventeen, and was responsible for the clearing of the Ashvale forest road that made Ashvale accessible to regular trade. She did not return. Ashvale named a road after her. Origia has not yet gotten around to it.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

CLOSING OBSERVATIONS

Origia is a village that has spent its entire existence as a beginning rather than an end. It has never produced a great monument, won a battle, or generated a legend of its own. It has generated, by the Bureau's conservative estimate, approximately two thousand legends — all of them belonging to people who started there and finished somewhere else.

This is perhaps its own kind of distinction.

The Bureau recommends that the village's true name — Origia — be restored to common usage, printed on maps, and taught in schools.

The Bureau acknowledges that this will probably not happen.

The inn is good. The beds are comfortable. The innkeeper will tell you the name of the village if you ask and will not hold it against you if you forget.

Her name is Hilda Thorpe.
The village is called Origia.
You will probably call it Starting Village.
