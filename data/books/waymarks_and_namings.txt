WAYMARKS AND NAMINGS
A Lexicon of Place Names in the Western Territories

Compiled and annotated by Scholar Petronis of the Cartographers' Guild
Third revised edition — with corrections to the second edition's embarrassing entry on Coldwater

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

FOREWORD TO THE THIRD EDITION

The Cartographers' Guild has, over the course of two centuries, produced more maps than any institution in the known world. In that time, we have been wrong about a remarkable number of things. The second edition of this lexicon listed Coldwater as "probably fictional." Coldwater's residents wrote to us. We have corrected this.

We have also, in this edition, finally addressed the Origia situation. See entry below.

This lexicon covers place names in the western territories only. Eastern territories are addressed in a separate volume compiled by Scholar-Merchant Katakalon, which I recommend with the caveat that Katakalon spells "Yixuan" four different ways in a single chapter and appears not to have noticed.

— Scholar Petronis, Cartographers' Guild

THE LEXICON

ASHVALE

A village of moderate size in the southern territories, notable primarily for its proximity to the Ashvale forest road and its annual harvest pilgrimage to Eldenmoor City. The name derives from the old word for ash-tree, combined with the valley designation "vale" — straightforward naming by practical people.

Ashvale's population is described in every survey as "stable," which in cartographic notation means it is neither growing nor shrinking and the residents seem to prefer it that way. The village produces smoked fish, woven baskets, and — according to one very specific trade record — an unusual quantity of beeswax.

The Ashvale pilgrim tradition, in which residents walk barefoot to Eldenmoor City each harvest season in honour of the First Sage Aetheryn (who was born in Ashvale), is well-documented. The Guild notes that Aetheryn is the village's most famous native, and that the village has never, to our knowledge, asked for anything in return for this. They simply walk. Every year. Barefoot.

We find this admirable.

CARTOGRAPHERS' NOTE: Ashvale appears on Guild maps under its correct name and has done so since the first edition. We have never called it anything else. The Guild is pleased to note this, as there are so few things we have consistently gotten right.

ELDENMOOR (CITY)

Eldenmoor City is the only settlement in the western territories to carry explicit mention in the Primordial Scriptures, where it appears first in the holy list of villages. Scholars of the Order of the Unbroken Seal have strong opinions about what this means. The Guild's position is that it means Eldenmoor was named first, which is either significant or arbitrary, and we decline to adjudicate.

The city was, historically, a village. Its growth into a walled city occurred over three centuries, driven primarily by pilgrimage traffic, the establishment of the Sagefire Spire, and what the city's own records describe as "the gradual realisation that people were going to keep coming regardless."

The Sagefire — an eternal flame burning atop the Spire — has become one of the western territories' most recognisable landmarks. The Guild's survey teams have used it as a navigation point since the second century. It has not, in recorded history, gone out.

See also: ORIGIA (for the Primordial Scriptures' second-named village).

ORIGIA

The correct name of the village most travellers, traders, adventurers, and mapmakers (including, historically, the Guild — see the first and second editions, for which we apologise) have called "Starting Village."

The village is named Origia. This is documented in:
- The Primordial Scriptures (second village named, after Eldenmoor)
- Bureau of Historical Records surveys, earliest dated to the second century of the Byzantra Empire
- The foundation stone of the old mill, which is carved in letters large enough to be read from the road
- Approximately nine hundred years of administrative records that everyone involved in mapmaking apparently chose not to consult

The Guild regrets that our first two editions listed this village as "Starting Village (local name: Origia)." The correct framing is: "Origia (colloquially known as Starting Village)." We have corrected this.

Why do people call it Starting Village? Because a very large number of legends, journeys, and notable adventures begin there. The roads from Origia connect to the forest paths, the ancient ruins, the northern wilderness, Ashvale, Thornwick, and — via those connections — eventually everywhere else. It is, structurally, a beginning.

It is also, to be precise, Origia. The innkeeper (current: Hilda Thorpe, descendant of a long line of innkeepers who have been correcting this misconception) will confirm this if asked. She has been confirming it for thirty years. She is tired. Please call it Origia.

CARTOGRAPHERS' NOTE: All Guild maps from this edition forward will list the village as Origia. We anticipate that people will continue to call it Starting Village. This is outside our jurisdiction.

THORNWICK

Thornwick is a settlement of scholarly character in the southwestern territories. Its library — modest by the standards of larger cities, substantial by the standards of everything in the western territories — has attracted academics, travellers, and correspondents for several centuries.

The village is named for the thornbushes that border its northern approach. This is, the Guild acknowledges, not particularly imaginative, but the naming is at least honest. The thorns are real. They are maintained. Nobody is misled.

Thornwick's most notable historical contribution is its role in the education of Aetheryn, the First Sage of the Primordial Scriptures, who spent two years there as a young scholar before continuing north to Eldenmoor. The library records from that period still exist and include a note from the head librarian, Brother Edwyn, describing Aetheryn as "exhausting, admirable, and unlikely to be satisfied by anything this library contains, though she is welcome to try." She tried. She moved on. Thornwick named a reading room after her approximately one hundred and fifty years later, once it was clear she was going to remain historically significant.

Thornwick maintains a formal scholarly relationship with Eldenmoor City, primarily consisting of regular letters requesting clarification on theological questions and receiving, in response, polite non-answers. This correspondence has been ongoing for two centuries. The scholars of Thornwick describe it as "productive." The keepers of the Sagefire Spire describe it as "tradition."

CARTOGRAPHERS' NOTE: Thornwick is listed on Guild maps under its correct name. The thorn bushes are noted as a landmark on the approach from the south. Travellers are advised to use the gate, not the hedgerow.

APPENDIX: NAMES THE GUILD HAS GOTTEN WRONG

For transparency, the Cartographers' Guild maintains a record of naming errors in previous editions. A partial list, western territories only:

- Origia listed as "Starting Village" (editions 1 and 2). Corrected in edition 3.
- Coldwater listed as "probably fictional" (edition 2). Coldwater is real. We apologise.
- The river now known as the Grey Run was listed as "Unnamed River (local designation disputed)." The locals call it the Grey Run. They have always called it the Grey Run. The dispute was internal to the Guild and should not have appeared in print.
- The village of Mirrenfen was omitted from edition 1 entirely. A reader from Mirrenfen wrote to inform us. The letter was six pages long and entirely justified.
- Eldenmoor was listed as a village in editions 1 and 2. It is a city. It has been a city for over a century. We acknowledge this was a significant oversight.

The Guild thanks all correspondents who have written to correct our errors over the years. We have read every letter. We have, where the evidence supported it, updated the maps.

Where the evidence did not support the correction — Coldwater notwithstanding — we have filed the letters and moved on.

This is, we believe, the correct approach to knowledge.

— Scholar Petronis
