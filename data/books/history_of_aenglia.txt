A HISTORY OF THE KINGDOM OF ÆNGLIA
From the First Settlements to the Present Reign of the Nevrolian Dynasty

By High Archivist Pellan of Eldenmoor
Second edition, revised and expanded
Commissioned by the Royal Court of Eldenmoor

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

FOREWORD

This history was first written at the request of King Aldric III of the Nevrolian line, who found the existing accounts of Ænglian history either too short, too wrong, or too flattering to be useful. I have attempted to write something that is none of those things. Whether I have succeeded is a question I leave to future archivists, who will no doubt find errors of their own to correct.

I have tried to give equal weight to the kingdom's genuine achievements and its genuine failures. A history that only records victories is not a history. It is a piece of decoration.

— High Archivist Pellan
  The Sagefire Archive, Eldenmoor

PART ONE: THE LAND BEFORE THE KINGDOM

Before there was a Kingdom of Ænglia, there were settlements.

The western territories were inhabited long before any king gave them a name. The first settlements — Origia, Eldenmoor, Ashvale, Crysthaven, Thornwick, and a dozen others whose names appear in the Primordial Scriptures or in the Bureau's oldest land records — were built by migrants moving inland from the coasts. They chose their sites for practical reasons: water, shelter, arable land, and roads, or the natural paths that would eventually become roads.

Of these early settlements, two would come to define the kingdom that eventually grew around them.

Origia, in the western lowlands, sat at the natural junction of every significant travel route in the region. Merchants, travellers, pilgrims, soldiers, and wanderers had no practical choice but to pass through it. It was not the largest settlement, nor the most defensible, nor the most prestigious. It was simply unavoidable. In the economics of a pre-kingdom landscape, this made it the most important village in the west.

Eldenmoor, to the northeast, was different. It was named first in the Primordial Scriptures — a fact that the Order of the Unbroken Seal will never allow anyone to forget — and it had, from its earliest recorded history, a quality that defies precise description. Scholars of the faith would say it was blessed. Scholars of political history would say it was well-positioned and well-led. Both are probably right.

When the First Sage Aetheryn chose Eldenmoor as her home — arriving from Ashvale, having studied in Thornwick, walking north through Crysthaven without stopping — she gave the village something no amount of geography could provide: a reason to matter beyond the merely practical. The Sagefire Spire, built over her grave, became a point of pilgrimage. Pilgrims became residents. Residents became a city. A city became a capital.

PART TWO: THE FOUNDING OF THE NEVROLIAN KINGDOM

The history of the Nevrolian dynasty begins with a woman.

Aelindra Nevrol was not born to rule anything. She was, by the accounts that survive her, a merchant's daughter from the coastal settlements who had a gift for negotiation and an apparently inexhaustible patience for people being wrong. She arrived in Eldenmoor in the year that historians now designate Year One of the Nevrolian Calendar, though no one called it that at the time.

What she found in Eldenmoor was a city that knew it was important but had not yet worked out what to do about it. The western settlements were loosely affiliated — trading with each other, sharing roads, occasionally feuding over water rights — but ungoverned in any formal sense. The Primordial Scriptures were the closest thing the region had to a shared framework, and the Scriptures, as this Archive has noted before, are more helpful on the subject of mountains and villages than they are on the subject of administrative law.

Aelindra spent seven years in Eldenmoor. She talked to merchants. She talked to farmers. She talked to the keepers of the Sagefire Spire, the scholars of Thornwick (by correspondence), the innkeepers of Origia (in person, which was more useful), and the elder councils of Ashvale. She then proposed, in a document that is now kept in the Archive's innermost vault, that the western settlements should form a kingdom.

The proposal was not immediately welcomed.

The negotiations that followed took four years. The arguments that had to be resolved included: who would lead, where the capital would be, what taxes would be levied, who would control the roads, whether Origia's market charter would be honoured, and — at considerable length — what the kingdom would be called. "Ænglia" was Aelindra's suggestion. It derives from an old word meaning "the land of those who cross" — a reference, scholars have argued, to the migratory origins of the western settlements, or to the trade routes that defined the region, or simply to the fact that everyone passed through it on the way to somewhere else. Probably all three.

Aelindra Nevrol was crowned the first Queen of Ænglia in Eldenmoor, beneath the Sagefire. The flame did not go out. Theologians still argue about whether this was meaningful. The Queen's documented response to this debate was: "It was already not going to go out. Proceed."

PART THREE: ORIGIA — THE KINGDOM'S CROSSROADS

The free market charter of Origia was one of the founding documents of the Kingdom of Ænglia. Queen Aelindra understood what the village was — a junction, a threshold, a place where the kingdom's blood moved — and she made a deliberate choice not to strangle it with lordship.

The charter declared Origia a free market settlement. No lord could levy private tolls on its roads. No guild could hold exclusive rights to its market square. Merchants of any origin, from any settlement in the kingdom or beyond, could trade there on equal terms under crown law. The crown's portion was a modest transit tax, collected by a royal administrator based in the village, and the rate was set deliberately below what any competitor route could offer.

The result was predictable: Origia became even more indispensable. The market grew. The roads in and out of the village were among the first in the kingdom to be formally maintained at crown expense. When the northern taiga opened to trade, the goods came through Origia. When Yixuan silk first reached the western territories, it was unloaded in Origia. When anyone in the kingdom needed to move anything valuable across the west, they passed through Origia.

Queen Aelindra's successors renewed the charter. Every monarch of the Nevrolian line has renewed it. The charter has outlasted six wars, three successions, one attempted coup, and a prolonged argument with the Merchant Guilds that this Archive prefers not to revisit. It is the single longest-standing legal instrument in the kingdom.

Origia's residents, it should be noted, are quietly aware of all this. They do not boast about it. They run their market, maintain their roads, and charge fair rates at the inn. They have been doing so for generations. The Kingdom of Ænglia depends on them, and they have never once demanded credit for it.

PART FOUR: THE BYZANTRA INCURSION

The Byzantra Empire's interest in Ænglian territory was, at its core, an interest in Origia.

Byzantran strategists were capable readers of trade routes. They understood that whoever held Origia held the western roads, and whoever held the western roads could tax every merchant moving goods between the coast, the forests, the capital, and the southern villages. It was not Eldenmoor's Sagefire they wanted, nor Ashvale's pilgrims, nor Thornwick's scholars. It was Origia's roads.

The first approach was diplomatic. A Byzantran envoy arrived during the reign of King Edric II with an offer of "protective inclusion" — the Empire's standard phrasing for arrangements that involved paying tribute and losing sovereignty. Lord Halvard of the Western March, who happened to be present, declined on the king's behalf with a brevity that the Archive has preserved verbatim: "Ænglia does not require Byzantine protection. Origia is ours. Good morning."

The second approach was military. Following King Edric II's death — an event the Empire appears to have anticipated and planned around — a Byzantran force crossed the western border during the interregnum and moved toward Origia.

They encountered three problems.

The first was the Ænglian border militia, which had been warned. The second was a company of mercenaries who happened to be staying at Origia's inn, between contracts, and who found the situation an acceptable opportunity for employment. The third was the terrain, which the Byzantrans had surveyed from maps — including, this Archive regrets to note, some maps produced by the Cartographers' Guild that were not entirely accurate regarding the western lowlands.

The Byzantran force retreated. The Nevrolian court filed a formal protest. The Empire did not attempt a third incursion.

Origia was never occupied. Origia was never administered by Byzantra. Origia was never, not for a single day, part of the Byzantra Empire.

The Archive notes this because certain maps — since corrected — implied otherwise. Let the record show: Origia was and remains Ænglian.

PART FIVE: THE NEVROLIAN DYNASTY TO THE PRESENT

The Nevrolian line has ruled the Kingdom of Ænglia from Eldenmoor for nine generations. A brief account of each reign would require a separate volume — which the Archive has, in fact, produced; it is available in the lower reading rooms — but the broad strokes are as follows.

The first three monarchs — Aelindra, her son Edric I, and his daughter Mara — consolidated the kingdom's internal structure, established the road maintenance system, and fought two defensive wars, both of which the kingdom won and neither of which it particularly wanted.

Edric II, the fourth monarch, extended the kingdom's northern trade connections and managed the Byzantran incursion already described. He died in his bed, which his court historians considered a notable achievement given the times.

The middle reigns were quieter. Ælfrith I, Aelindra II (named for the founder), and Edric III expanded the kingdom's administrative reach into the eastern reaches, established the legal academy at Thornwick, and produced, between them, sufficient legislation to occupy the Archive's scholars for the following two centuries.

The current reign — at the time of this edition's completion — is that of King Aldric III, who commissioned this work and has thus far proven a capable if impatient monarch with a particular interest in accurate record-keeping. He has also, this Archive notes for the record, renewed the Origia free market charter in his first year of reign, as every Nevrolian monarch has done before him.

The kingdom continues.

PART SIX: ELDENMOOR — THE CAPITAL

Eldenmoor City is what happens when a village that matters becomes a city that knows it.

Three centuries of Nevrolian rule have given Eldenmoor walls, a palace, a court, a legal archive, a military garrison, three market districts, and a pilgrimage infrastructure that supports thousands of visitors each year. The Sagefire Spire remains its most recognisable landmark. The royal palace is larger. The Spire is more famous. The Keeper of the Sagefire and the crown's chamberlain have an arrangement about which of them controls which functions, and neither will discuss it with the Archive.

Eldenmoor also houses the Bureau of Historical Records, the Cartographers' Guild's western office, the Order of the Unbroken Seal's primary chapter house, and — since the current reign — a permanent delegation from the Merchant Council of Origia, whose members attend court on trade matters and have been known to stay for months.

The city's relationship to the rest of the kingdom is defined by what it is: the place where the kingdom is decided. Laws are made here. Disputes are settled here. Treaties are signed here. The king sits here, and the Sagefire burns above it all, unchanging, as it has since Aetheryn's death.

Pilgrims come from Ashvale every harvest. Scholars come from Thornwick with questions. Merchants come from Origia with concerns. The court receives all of them, in its own time and order, as courts do.

CLOSING NOTE

The Kingdom of Ænglia is not the largest kingdom in the known world. It is not the oldest. It has not won the most wars or produced the most celebrated heroes. What it has done is persist — through incursions, successions, and the ordinary grinding difficulty of governing a territory where the most important village refuses to be called by its correct name.

The kingdom endures. Eldenmoor burns. Origia's market opens every morning.

These things, in this archivist's view, are sufficient.

— High Archivist Pellan
  The Sagefire Archive, Eldenmoor
  Year 312 of the Nevrolian Calendar
