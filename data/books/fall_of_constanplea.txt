THE FALL OF CONSTANPLEA
Being a True Account of the Last Days of the City of Light
As Witnessed and Written by Scholar Theophanes, Keeper of the Imperial Library
In the Year of the City's Doom

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PREFACE

I write these words in exile, on the shore of a sea that once belonged to us. The ink smells of smoke. I do not think the smell will ever leave me.

Constanplea was not merely a city. It was the memory of civilization — every library, every cathedral, every column and cobblestone laid down across a thousand years. It was the idea that human beings could build something lasting. When the walls fell, more than stone came down.

I survived. I do not know if that is a blessing or a punishment.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART ONE: THE CITY IN ITS GLORY

For one thousand years, Constanplea stood where the land of our Empire met the sea. It was the capital of Byzantra — the old empire, the great empire, the empire that outlasted every barbarian horde that broke against its walls.

Those walls. Theodosian walls, three layers of them, twenty feet thick at the base. Towers every fifty paces. The city within them was a world unto itself. The Church of the Eternal Flame dominated the skyline — its great dome, they said, was held up not by columns but by God's own breath. When sunlight struck through its upper windows at midday, gold seemed to pour down from heaven itself.

The markets of Constanplea traded goods from every corner of the known world. Silks from Yixuan, spices from the distant east, furs from the northern steppes, amber from the frozen shores. In the great Hippodrome, citizens of every nation cheered and argued and lived. The scholars at the Academy preserved texts that the rest of the world had forgotten.

Basileus Konstantinos IX was our last emperor. A good man. Brave. He knew, I think, even before the siege began that the end had come. The empire had been shrinking for generations — nibbled away at the edges by Osman cavalry raids, weakened by civil wars, abandoned by the western kingdoms who once called us brothers. When the final army of Sultan Yildirim assembled outside the walls, we were perhaps eight thousand soldiers to defend a city built for five hundred thousand.

Konstantinos rode the walls every night. He spoke to every soldier he could find. He slept, from what I heard, very little.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART TWO: THE SIEGE

The Osman host arrived in the final month of winter. We heard them before we saw them — the drums, the thousands upon thousands of feet, and then the thunder of the great cannons being dragged into position.

Those cannons were something new under the sun. Master Orban had built them — a foreign engineer, a man who had first offered his services to Basileus Konstantinos, and when we could not pay his price, had sold himself to the Sultan instead. History turns on such moments of poverty.

The cannons fired at the walls day and night. The ground shook. The city trembled. Stones that had stood for six hundred years crumbled. We repaired the breaches through the night with earth and timber and prayer, and in the morning the cannons began again.

Seven weeks. The siege lasted seven weeks. We starved. We prayed. We waited for the ships from the west that never came — the kingdoms of the world had made their calculations and decided that Constanplea was already dead.

In the end, it was a gate that doomed us. The Kerkoporta — a small postern gate in the northern wall, left unlocked by accident or by treachery, to this day no one knows. A column of Osman soldiers found it in the grey hours before dawn, slipped through, and raised the Sultan's banners on the walls above.

When the defenders saw those banners, something broke. Not all at once, but like a dam giving way — first a crack, then everything.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART THREE: THE FALL

I was in the library when the city fell. I remember the sound — not the cannons, which had been constant, but the sound when they stopped. The sudden silence was worse than any bombardment. Then the screaming began.

Basileus Konstantinos IX died that morning. He tore the insignia from his cloak, so that his body could not be identified and displayed, and charged into the Osman soldiers at the broken wall with a handful of his guard. He was never seen again. Some say they found his body later, identified only by the imperial eagles embroidered on his boots. I do not know if this is true. I hope it is. A man like that deserved to be found.

I escaped through the sea walls with a satchel of manuscripts. The fire was already spreading through the scholars' quarter when I left. Three days later, from a boat far out in the strait, I watched the smoke rise. It burned for a long time.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART FOUR: THE NAMING

Sultan Yildirim entered the city three days after its fall. By all accounts, he rode alone to the Church of the Eternal Flame, dismounted, and knelt to pray in it. He commanded the looting to stop. He commanded that the surviving population — those not taken as slaves — be left in peace.

He was, by all accounts, a man of his word on this. Order was restored. The killing stopped.

And then he renamed the city.

Constanplea — City of Konstantine, jewel of Byzantra, light of the civilized world — became Kostantora. The name was close enough to feel like a taunt, different enough to mean: the old world is finished. Something new lives here now.

The Church of the Eternal Flame became a house of the Osman faith. Its mosaics were whitewashed. Its bells were melted down for cannons. Its priests were given a month to leave or convert.

Most left.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

EPILOGUE

I am old now. I have copies of some of what I saved — fragments, really, of a library that contained the accumulated knowledge of a thousand years. I teach what I can to those who will listen.

Kostantora is, I am told, a magnificent city under its new masters. The markets are full again. The harbour bristles with ships. The Osmans have built great monuments on the bones of ours.

Perhaps this is how it always goes. One civilization absorbs another. The names change. The stones remain.

But I remember the dome of the Church of the Eternal Flame on a winter morning, with gold light falling through its windows.

I remember what we had.

I will not let myself forget.

— Theophanes, Scholar of Byzantra, writing in exile
  Three years after the Fall of Constanplea
