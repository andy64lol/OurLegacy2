ORIGINS OF THE OSMAN PEOPLE
A Study of the Steppe Confederations and the Rise of the Kalovanim Empire
Compiled by Imperial Archivist Demetrios Palaiologos, Byzantra Academy of History
Second Edition, Revised and Expanded

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

INTRODUCTION

Of all the peoples who have troubled the borders of the civilized world, none has risen so far, so fast, as the Kalovars. A century ago, they were a handful of clans scratching a living from the grasslands north of Yixuan. Today, their empire extends from those same steppes to the shores of the Inner Sea. They destroyed Byzantra, the oldest empire in the known world, and built something new in its place.

To understand how this happened is not merely an academic exercise. It is a matter of survival.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART ONE: THE STEPPES OF NORTH YIXUAN

The lands north of the great Yixuan kingdom are vast and brutal — an ocean of grass that stretches to the horizon in every direction. In winter, the wind from the north can kill an exposed man in hours. In summer, the sun bakes the earth to clay. It is not a place that invites settlement. It is a place that produces warriors.

The earliest historical records from Yixuan merchants describe the steppe peoples as divided into dozens of clans, each controlling grazing territories and water sources, each in a state of near-constant low-level warfare with its neighbours. They rode horses bred for endurance rather than speed, kept great herds of cattle and sheep, and followed the seasons across the grassland.

They were not, contrary to what the court scholars of Byzantra wished to believe, savages. Their oral traditions were rich and complex. Their laws — called the Törü — governed disputes, alliances, and blood-price with remarkable sophistication. Their astronomical knowledge, developed from centuries of navigating by stars across a land without landmarks, was in some respects more advanced than ours.

But they had no cities. No roads. No written language. And because they had none of these things, we did not take them seriously until it was far too late.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART TWO: THE GREAT KHAN OSMAN

The figure who changed everything was a man named Kalovar ibn Vethrak, born in a clan encampment somewhere on the northern steppe, date unknown. The Kalovars themselves claim divine favour for his birth — that a great eagle was seen circling overhead when he first drew breath, and that the clan elders prophesied he would rule all the lands between the steppes and the sea.

Prophecy or not, what Kalovar actually possessed was a combination of qualities rarely found in one person: physical courage that inspired fanatical personal loyalty, political intelligence that let him manipulate clan rivalries to his benefit, and a religious conviction — he had converted to the faith of the desert prophets — that gave his warriors the sense of fighting for something larger than themselves.

He began by unifying the clans of his immediate region. This alone was unprecedented. The steppe clans had fought each other for generations; clan identity ran deeper than any other loyalty. Kalovar managed it through a combination of marriage alliances, battlefield demonstration, and the shrewd promise that under his leadership, there was far more to be gained from raiding the wealthy lands to the west than from fighting each other over the same grassland.

Within twenty years of his rise to prominence, Kalovar commanded an army of thirty thousand mounted warriors. No single clan could field a tenth of that number. He had, in effect, created a nation from a scattered collection of feuding families.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART THREE: THE WESTERN MARCH

The decision to move west was strategic and inevitable. The northern steppes, while vast, offered limited resources beyond what the Kalovars already possessed. To the east lay the vast domains of Yixuan — too powerful to challenge directly, and too useful as a trading partner to antagonise. To the south, the desert.

To the west lay Byzantra.

The first Kalovar raids across the Byzantran border were probing attacks — testing the response time of our border garrisons, identifying fortified positions, learning the road networks. Our commanders at the time reported them as routine bandit raids, a nuisance but not a threat. This assessment proved catastrophically wrong.

By the time Kalovar himself died — he did not live to see the fall of Constanplea, but he saw enough — his confederation had absorbed or destroyed a dozen smaller kingdoms along Byzantra's eastern frontier. His son, and his son's son, continued the push westward, each generation pressing further into what had once been imperial territory.

The Kalovar military system was formidable. At its core were the light cavalry — riders who could cover sixty miles in a day, who could shoot a recurve bow accurately at full gallop, who could live off the land for weeks without a supply train. Around this core, the Kalovars built an increasingly sophisticated military machine: siege engineers, infantry corps drawn from conquered peoples (the Kazharis), artillery. They were not merely raiders. They were conquerors who learned.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART FOUR: THE EMPIRE IS BORN

The formal proclamation of the Kalovanim Empire — "the Empire of the House of Kalovar" — occurred in the generation before Yildirim. By that point, the transformation from steppe confederation to settled empire was well advanced. The Kalovars had adopted writing. They had built administrative systems. They had established a capital city, then another, and then — with the fall of Constanplea — the grandest capital of all.

The steppe culture did not disappear, but it was buried beneath layers of imperial ceremony, religious practice, and the practical necessities of governing millions of people. The old clan loyalties were replaced by loyalty to the Sultan and to the faith. The Törü was partially absorbed into the new imperial law code.

Yildirim — Sultan Velkar, "The Lightning," named for the speed of his campaign — was the man who completed what his great-grandfather had begun. He was, by all accounts, a man of significant personal qualities: physically imposing, militarily gifted, genuinely pious, and possessed of a political intelligence that rivalled Kalovar himself.

He took Constanplea in the fifty-third year of his life. He died twenty years later, having expanded the empire in four additional directions. His dynasty continues.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

CONCLUSION

The rise of the Kalovars from steppe confederation to world empire in less than two centuries is without parallel in recorded history. They were aided by the weakness of their enemies — Byzantra most of all — but weakness alone does not explain their success. They were also genuinely formidable: militarily innovative, politically sophisticated, and sustained by a religious conviction that transformed individual soldiers into instruments of what they understood as divine will.

Whether their empire endures as long as Byzantra's did remains to be seen.

Byzantra lasted a thousand years.

The Kalovars have had a hundred so far.

The world turns. Empires rise and fall.

Whether something better rises in their place is a question none of us alive today will live to answer.

— Demetrios Palaiologos, Archivist, writing in the thirty-first year of the reign of Sultan Velkar
