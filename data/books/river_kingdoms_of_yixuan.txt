THE RIVER KINGDOMS OF YIXUAN
A Geographic and Cultural Survey of the Eastern Continent
Compiled by the Byzantra Academy of Geography and Natural Philosophy
Third Edition, Revised by Scholar Periandros of Nikomedeia

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

INTRODUCTION

Yixuan is not a single kingdom. This is the first and most important thing a western traveller must understand. When merchants speak of "Yixuan" as though it were one place, they are using a name — the name the great river dynasty carved into stone some seven centuries ago — to describe an entire continent's worth of civilizations, climates, peoples, and histories.

The name comes from the Yi Xuan River: a river so vast that in its upper reaches it is two miles wide and in its lower delta spans nearly twenty. Everything in the eastern lands flows toward or away from this river. It is the axis of the world there, as the Inner Sea is the axis of ours.

To understand Yixuan, you must understand the river.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART ONE: THE LAND AND ITS WATERS

The Yi Xuan River rises in the mountains called the Spine of Heaven — a range so tall that the peaks are perpetually lost in cloud and have, as far as anyone knows, never been climbed by a living person. From those heights the river flows east and south for thousands of miles before emptying into the Eastern Ocean through a delta so broad and fertile that the land seems to be made of water and the water of land.

Along this river and its tributaries lie the great kingdoms:

THE JADE COAST KINGDOMS (the Sunlit Princes)
The wealthiest and most populous of the eastern realms, built on trade. Their cities are famous throughout the known world — tier upon tier of architecture climbing the hillsides above the river, lacquered in red and gold, their harbours thick with ships. The Sunlit Princes (they call their rulers by this title) have maintained a loose confederation for four centuries, trading with each other when convenient and warring when necessary.

THE INNER PROVINCES
The agricultural heartland. Rice paddies stretch from horizon to horizon, flooded in the wet season, golden in the dry. The people of the inner provinces are considered by coastal Yixuans to be provincial and behind the times; the people of the inner provinces consider the coastal kingdoms decadent and spiritually hollow. Both assessments have some truth.

THE NORTHERN MOUNTAIN STATES
Smaller kingdoms clinging to the lower slopes of the Spine of Heaven. They are miners and craftsmen primarily — the iron and copper that feeds the eastern foundries comes mostly from here. They have a reputation for stubbornness that their history amply justifies: several attempted conquests of the mountain states across the centuries have failed expensively.

THE SOUTHERN WETLANDS
A collection of city-states built on the river delta. The richest trading posts in the known world — goods from three continents pass through these harbours. The climate is hot, wet, and said by northerners to be absolutely intolerable; the locals consider it pleasant.

PART TWO: THE PEOPLE

The people of Yixuan are not one people any more than the people of the western kingdoms are one people. A resident of the Jade Coast looks, speaks, eats, and thinks differently from someone raised in the inner provinces — and both of them are quite different from the mountain-kingdom peoples or the delta traders.

What they share is the river and the writing system that the ancient dynasty imposed across the continent seven centuries ago. A scholar from the Jade Coast and a merchant from the southern delta cannot understand each other's spoken language, but they can read the same written characters. This has held a diverse civilization together through upheavals that, in the west, would have produced a dozen entirely separate cultures.

The eastern societies are, as a general observation, more hierarchical than western ones in some ways and more meritocratic in others. Birth matters less than it does in Byzantra's aristocracy — the great examination system rewards intelligence and hard work regardless of family. But once rank is achieved, it is defended with a ferocity that western nobility would recognize immediately.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PART THREE: WHAT WE IMPORT, WHAT WE DO NOT UNDERSTAND

From Yixuan comes silk — the finest in the world, produced by a method that the eastern kingdoms have guarded for centuries. Whoever invented the process of extracting thread from silkworm cocoons and weaving it into cloth of such quality deserves more praise than history gives them. Several western merchants have attempted to steal the method. None have succeeded, or at least none have succeeded completely.

Also from Yixuan: paper (their method is superior to ours), ceramic work of extraordinary quality, lacquerware, medicines unknown in western pharmacies, spices that command astonishing prices by the time they reach our markets, and a black powder substance that some of their military engineers use for fortification work. The last item is something our scholars are very interested in understanding better.

What we export east: wool, amber, glass (they find our glass-blowing technique curious but have their own quite different tradition), furs from the northern steppes, and increasingly, Kalovar leatherwork, which the eastern markets seem to find exotic.

PART FOUR: ON VISITING

If you are reading this book planning to travel east, be advised:

The journey by sea around the southern coast takes approximately four months in good conditions and considerably longer in bad ones. The overland routes through the steppe are faster but depend entirely on Kalovar goodwill for safe passage.

Upon arrival, hire a translator immediately. Do not attempt to operate without one. The languages of the eastern coast are tonal — a word spoken at the wrong pitch means something entirely different from the same word spoken correctly. This has produced misunderstandings ranging from the embarrassing to the diplomatic.

The food is extraordinary. Eat everything offered to you. Ask questions afterward.

Do not make jokes about the Emperor. Any Emperor, current or historical.

APPENDIX: PRINCIPAL CITIES

RIVER'S CROWN (called Jianguan by its inhabitants): The largest city in the known world by most estimates. Population unknown but certainly in the hundreds of thousands. The Imperial Palace occupies an entire district. The market quarter never closes.

GOLDEN HARBOUR (Jinshin): The primary trading port on the Jade Coast. All major western merchant houses maintain a presence here. The foreign quarter is extensive and has its own guards, courts, and taverns that serve food identifiable to a western palate.

MOONRISE CITY (Yuécheng): The cultural and scholarly capital of the northern mountain states. The library here is said to rival the old Academy of Constanplea. I have not been able to verify this personally but I have met scholars who speak of it with reverence.

— Scholar Periandros, dictated in Golden Harbour after two years of eastern travel
